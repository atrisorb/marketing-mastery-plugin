# Pricing Strategy

Price is the only marketing-mix lever that directly captures value instead of creating cost — a 1% price improvement usually moves profit more than a 1% volume or cost improvement. Yet pricing is often abdicated to finance and set at cost-plus in a short-term, reactive way. This skill sets price deliberately from all three angles, defends margin against discount leakage, and adapts price across the lifecycle.

## Trigger

User asks to set a price, run a break-even, choose a launch price (skim/penetration), analyze margins/discounts, find willingness to pay, justify a premium, respond to a competitor's price, or design packaging/tiers. Also trigger when pricing is being done purely on cost.

## Inputs

1. **Offering and its value proposition** — the value ceiling (pull `value-proposition-design`)
2. **Cost structure** — fixed costs, variable/unit cost (the floor)
3. **Competitor prices** and the customer's alternatives
4. **Objective** — max profit, share, skim, deter entry, image, survival
5. **Lifecycle stage** and **business model** (one-off vs. subscription changes everything)
6. **Any WTP data** — survey responses, historical price tests

## Process

### 1. Anchor the pricing corridor

Fix the boundaries before choosing a number:
- **Floor = cost** (below this you lose money; use variable cost for short-run decisions, total cost for the long run).
- **Ceiling = total economic value to the customer** (above this the best alternative wins).
- The chosen price lives between; where in the corridor depends on the objective and orientation.

### 2. Work all three orientations, then triangulate

Never rely on one. Compute each, then reconcile.

**A. Cost-based** (simple, internal, but blind to market)
- *Markup/cost-plus:* price = unit cost × (1 + markup), or price = unit cost / (1 − desired margin%).
- *Target-ROI:* price to earn a required return on invested capital at a planned volume.
- *Break-even:* the volume where you neither profit nor lose.
  - Contribution margin per unit: `m = price − variable cost`
  - Break-even volume: `Q_BE = Fixed Costs / m`
  - To hit a target profit: `Q = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) / m`
  - Use it to test "at this price, what volume must we sell, and is that realistic?"
- Weakness: ignores competitors and customers; can under- or over-price badly. Use as a floor check, not the answer.

**B. Competition-based** (market-aware, but can commoditize)
- Position relative to rivals: follower (track the leader), parallel (tacit non-aggression), premium (if differentiated), or aggressive/predatory (only if you own a durable cost/tech advantage).
- Understand *why* competitors price as they do (their strategy, cost base, and financial health) before matching or moving — copying a rival's mistake is still a mistake.
- Reserve price wars for cases where you are the clear cost leader; they are usually negative-sum and reset customer price expectations downward permanently.

**C. Value / market-based** (best for margin, needs insight)
- Price to the customer's *perceived value*, working "backwards" from what they'll pay to the maximum acceptable cost. Value is individual, subjective, and perception-driven — for both consumer and industrial goods.
- Methods: value quantification (from `value-proposition-design`), price-quality tiering, price discrimination (a tailored price per segment/customer by their value, not your cost), and psychological pricing (odd/charm pricing, round-number signaling, reference-price framing).

### 3. Measure willingness to pay (when needed)

If value is uncertain, research WTP rather than guess:
- **Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter** — ask four questions (too cheap / cheap-bargain / expensive / too expensive) across a sample; the intersections reveal an acceptable price range and the optimal price point. Cross-reference the resulting price with the unit cost to confirm the margin works, and re-test if cost changes.
- **Gabor-Granger** — test purchase intent at ascending prices to find the revenue-maximizing point.
- **Conjoint / choice** — isolate the value (and price elasticity) of individual features.

### 4. Set lifecycle and launch pricing

- **Skimming** — launch high, harvest early value-insensitive segments, then step down. Fits genuine innovation, patent/lead protection, capacity limits, strong brand.
- **Penetration** — launch low to build volume, share, and cost-curve advantage fast, deterring entrants. Fits price-sensitive mass markets and network/scale businesses.
- Across the lifecycle, migrate: introduction (follow value-rich segments, skim or penetrate) → growth (ease as competitors arrive, expand segments) → maturity (defend, tactical promotions, discipline) → decline (harvest or defensive cuts).

### 5. Protect the pocket price (stop the leakage)

List price is rarely what you keep. Build the **price waterfall** from list to pocket price:

`List price − on-invoice discounts − off-invoice leaks (rebates, promo, freight, terms/financing cost, payment delays, returns, co-op, special deals) = Pocket Price`

- Compute the pocket price per key account/segment and find where margin leaks unnoticed. Small, uncontrolled off-invoice items compound into large margin loss.
- Distinguish deliberate, earned discounts (volume, functional, cash, seasonal — each tied to a purpose) from hidden, unmanaged leaks. Reclaim the leaks first; they're pure margin.
- Track pocket price as the real KPI, not list price.

### 6. Adapt with dynamic / yield management (where it fits)

For perishable, fixed-capacity, segmentable-demand businesses (airlines, hotels, events, cloud capacity, SaaS overages), use **yield/revenue management**: sell the right unit, to the right customer, at the right time, for the right price. Preconditions: relatively fixed capacity, perishable inventory, segmentable demand, ability to sell in advance, demand fluctuation, and variable costs low vs. fixed. This is value-based pricing made continuous.

### 7. Channel and list structure

Set prices coherently across the channel: each intermediary marks up, so the end-customer price and each partner's margin must be planned together. A strong brand/position lets you hold price to the end customer; a weak position forces aggressive, push-oriented pricing. Decide list architecture (national/regional, by channel, discount schedules) and what stays on-list vs. in other buckets.

## Output

### Pricing Corridor
Floor (cost), ceiling (value), and the objective steering the choice within.

### Three-Orientation Analysis
Cost-based result (incl. break-even math), competition-based read, and value/WTP-based result — with the triangulated recommended price.

### Launch / Lifecycle Pricing
Skim vs. penetration decision (if launching) and the price migration path by stage.

### Pocket-Price Defense
The waterfall for key segments/accounts, leaks identified, and the margin-recovery actions.

### Structure & Dynamics
List/discount architecture, channel pricing, and any dynamic/yield opportunity.

### Recommendation
The number(s), the rationale, the expected margin and break-even, and the risks.

## Follow-up

Ask: "Would you like me to:
- Model break-even and profit across a range of prices and volumes?
- Design the discount/tier/packaging structure in detail?
- Analyze pocket price for specific accounts or channels?
- Align pricing with the rest of the marketing mix?"
