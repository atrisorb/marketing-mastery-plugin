# Market & Environment Analysis

Strategy is the fit between what the outside world offers and threatens (external) and what the firm can actually do (internal). This skill produces that fit analysis end-to-end: macro-environment → industry structure → internal capability → SWOT → TOWS actions. The output is not a list of adjectives; it is a set of strategic moves justified by structure.

## Trigger

User asks for a situation analysis, market/industry analysis, competitive structure, market-entry assessment, SWOT, TOWS, Porter's Five Forces, value chain, or the analytical base for a plan. Also trigger when a strategy request lacks any structural grounding — do the analysis first.

## Inputs

Gather what's available; research or reason through the rest and label assumptions.

1. **Focal firm/brand and its offering**
2. **Market / industry boundary** — define it explicitly (product scope, geography, customer type). Most bad analyses come from a fuzzy market definition.
3. **Purpose** — entry decision, defend position, annual plan input, investment case
4. **Known competitors, suppliers, customers, substitutes** (if any)
5. **B2B or B2C** — changes buyer-power dynamics and value-chain emphasis

If tools are connected, use web search / an SEO or market-intelligence MCP to pull competitor, funding, and market-size signals; otherwise reason from what the user provides and flag confidence.

## Process

Work the layers outside-in, then turn inward, then synthesize. Keep every claim tied to evidence or a stated assumption.

### 1. Macro-environment scan (PESTEL / STEEP)

Scan the forces the firm cannot control but must anticipate. Use PESTEL (or the STEEP variant); for each factor capture the trend, its direction, and the *so-what* for this brand — an unactioned factor is noise.

| Factor | What to examine | Capture |
|--------|----------------|---------|
| Political | Regulation risk, trade policy, government stability, procurement rules | Trend + implication |
| Economic | Growth, rates, inflation, FX, purchasing power, industry cycle | Trend + implication |
| Social | Demographics, values, lifestyles, sustainability expectations | Trend + implication |
| Technological | Enabling/disruptive tech, digitalization, automation, R&D pace | Trend + implication |
| Environmental | Resource scarcity, emissions rules, circularity pressure | Trend + implication |
| Legal | Liability, IP, data/privacy, labor, standards | Trend + implication |

Rate each factor's impact (high/med/low) and urgency (now / 1-3 yr / horizon). Carry the high-impact ones into SWOT as opportunities or threats.

### 2. Industry structure — Porter's Five Forces

Assess how the structure of the industry sets the *ceiling on profitability*. For each force, judge strength (low/medium/high) with the reasoning, then state what it means for strategy.

| Force | Stronger (worse for incumbents) when… | Implication to note |
|-------|--------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Rivalry among competitors | Many equal players, slow growth, high fixed costs, low differentiation, high exit barriers | Basis to differentiate or consolidate |
| Threat of new entrants | Low capital needs, weak brands, easy channel access, no scale economies, low switching costs | Where to build barriers |
| Bargaining power of buyers | Few large buyers, undifferentiated product, low switching cost, buyer can backward-integrate, price transparency | Lock-in and value levers |
| Bargaining power of suppliers | Concentrated suppliers, unique inputs, high switching cost, supplier can forward-integrate | Sourcing/vertical strategy |
| Threat of substitutes | Attractive price-performance alternatives, low switching cost, changing needs | Innovation urgency |

Add a read on **complementors / ecosystem** where relevant (platforms, APIs, integrators) — for digital and B2B businesses the sixth "force" of complementarity often dominates. Conclude with the overall attractiveness verdict and *where the profit pools sit*.

### 3. Internal audit — Porter's Value Chain

Map how the firm creates margin, activity by activity, to locate real strengths and cost/differentiation drivers. For each activity note whether it is a source of **competitive advantage**, **parity**, or **weakness**.

Primary activities:
- **Inbound logistics** — sourcing, receiving, storing inputs
- **Operations** — transforming inputs into the offering
- **Outbound logistics** — distribution and delivery
- **Marketing & sales** — demand generation, channels, pricing, brand
- **Service** — installation, support, retention, success

Support activities:
- **Firm infrastructure** — management, finance, quality systems
- **Human resource management** — recruiting, skills, incentives
- **Technology development** — R&D, process/product tech, digital
- **Procurement** — buying practices across the firm

Link the chain to advantage: a genuine advantage is an activity (or a linkage between activities) that is valuable, rare, hard to imitate, and organized to exploit (VRIO). Flag which strengths pass VRIO — those are the ones strategy should be built on.

### 4. Synthesize — weighted SWOT

Consolidate into SWOT, drawing Opportunities/Threats from steps 1-2 and Strengths/Weaknesses from step 3. Avoid the classic failure of a flat, unprioritized list:

- Keep each item **specific and evidenced** ("38% share in Area 3 vs. 25% category average," not "good market position").
- **Weight** each item high/medium/low by impact.
- Ensure Strengths/Weaknesses are *internal and relative to competitors*; Opportunities/Threats are *external*. Misfiling is the most common SWOT error — police it.

### 5. Convert to action — TOWS matrix

A SWOT that stops at four lists is inert. Cross the factors to generate strategies:

| | Strengths (S) | Weaknesses (W) |
|---|---|---|
| **Opportunities (O)** | **SO — Attack:** use strengths to seize opportunities | **WO — Build:** fix/partner to capture opportunities |
| **Threats (T)** | **ST — Defend:** use strengths to neutralize threats | **WT — Avoid/Retreat:** minimize exposure, exit, or divest |

For each populated cell, write 1-3 concrete strategic options. Then prioritize across all cells by impact × feasibility, and mark the 3-5 moves that make the shortlist.

## Output

### Executive Summary
3-5 sentences: industry attractiveness verdict, the firm's defensible advantage (if any), the single biggest opportunity and biggest threat.

### Macro Scan (PESTEL/STEEP)
Table of high-impact factors with direction and implication (drop the low-impact ones to an appendix).

### Industry Structure (Five Forces)
Force-by-force strength rating with reasoning + overall attractiveness and where profit concentrates.

### Internal Audit (Value Chain + VRIO)
Where the firm has advantage / parity / weakness; which strengths pass VRIO.

### Weighted SWOT
Four quadrants, each item weighted and evidenced.

### TOWS Strategic Options
Populated matrix + a prioritized shortlist of 3-5 moves, each with rationale and rough impact/feasibility.

### Confidence & Gaps
What would sharpen the analysis (data to pull, assumptions to test).

## Follow-up

Ask: "Would you like me to:
- Turn the TOWS shortlist into a sequenced marketing plan?
- Deep-dive the competitive set into positioning maps and battlecards?
- Pressure-test the market-entry case with sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM)?
- Feed this into the brand lifecycle diagnosis to set stage-appropriate priorities?"
