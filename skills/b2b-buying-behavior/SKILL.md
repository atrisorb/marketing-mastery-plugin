# B2B Buying Behavior

Selling to a business is not selling to a person — it is selling to a *decision-making unit* running a *procedural process* under *uncertainty and derived demand*. Industrial markets are concentrated, heterogeneous, continuous, and change-driven, which makes relationships and interdependence central. This skill decodes how the customer organization buys so the seller can align to it and win.

## Trigger

User sells B2B/industrial and needs to understand the buying center/DMU, buying process, procurement logic (buy-classes, Kraljic), key-account relationships, or how to influence an organizational purchase. Also trigger for any B2B GTM, targeting, or sales-enablement question.

## Inputs

1. **What you sell** and to which type of customer (end-user firm, OEM, installer, distributor)
2. **Deal type** — first-time, repeat, or a re-opened decision
3. **What's known about the customer's organization** and current supplier
4. **Where you are** — incumbent defending or challenger entering
5. **The value/uncertainty at stake** for the buyer

## Process

### 1. Recognize what makes B2B different

Anchor the analysis in the structural differences from consumer marketing, because they drive everything else:
- **Derived demand** — B2B demand descends from the customer's own downstream demand; it's often volatile and amplified up the chain. You may need to stimulate the *end* demand to move your buyer.
- **Concentration** — few, large, important buyers (Pareto: a handful can be most of the business). The single customer carries weight.
- **Professional, proceduralized buying** — trained buyers, formal processes, specifications, and often tenders/gare.
- **Continuity & interdependence** — long, relational exchanges with high switching costs and mutual investment.
- **The customer is a system of people**, not one decider.

### 2. Map the buying center (DMU) and its roles

The firm must satisfy a *unit*, not a person. Identify who plays each role (roles can be split across people/functions, and one person can hold several):

| Role | Who they are | What wins them |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| Initiator | Recognizes the need/problem | A framed problem worth solving |
| Influencer | Shapes criteria and specs (often technical) | Credible expertise, proof, standards |
| Decider | Makes the actual choice | Business case, risk reduction |
| Buyer | Executes the purchase, negotiates | Terms, reliability, ease |
| User | Uses the product/service | Usability, support, outcomes |
| Gatekeeper | Controls information/access | Relevance, respect for their process |

For each role, note their goals, criteria, and the message/proof that moves them — and how you'll reach the ones your salesperson can't (this is where communication and content do work personal selling can't).

### 3. Classify the buy-class

The buying situation dictates effort, players, and opportunity:

| Buy-class | Characteristics | Incumbent play | Challenger play |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Straight rebuy | Routine, approved list, buyer decides alone | Automate reorder; maintain quality/service; make switching pointless | Hard to enter; find dissatisfaction; small trial orders |
| Modified rebuy | Re-examined terms/specs/suppliers; more people involved | Defend actively; reinforce relationship | Real opening; propose improvements; aim to get on the list |
| New task | First-time, high risk, many people, long process, much information sought | Shape specs early; reduce risk | Biggest opening; use best salespeople; supply information and technical help early |

Getting written into the specification early in a new task is often the decisive move.

### 4. Work the buying process stage by stage

Organizational buying runs a sequence; meet the customer at each stage with the right support:
1. Need/problem recognition (anticipated or emergent)
2. Determination of characteristics/quantity needed
3. Specification of the product and criteria
4. Search for qualified suppliers
5. Acquisition and analysis of proposals
6. Evaluation and supplier selection
7. Purchase / order routine
8. Performance review and feedback

Map your actions to each stage; the earlier you engage, the more you shape criteria in your favor.

### 5. Reduce the buyer's perceived uncertainty

Buyers hesitate under three uncertainties; the supplier's job is to *reduce* them (this is a core source of preference):
- **Need uncertainty** — is the requirement well understood? → help define the need, provide information, propose solutions interactively.
- **Market uncertainty** — how do suppliers differ and change? → be proactive, credible, and transparent about differentiation.
- **Transaction uncertainty** — will delivery/quality/terms hold? → focus on delivery reliability, formalized terms, and managerial/organizational competence.

### 6. Apply procurement logic (Kraljic)

Buyers segment *their* purchases; anticipate how they see *you* to predict their behavior. The **Kraljic matrix** crosses profit impact (importance) with supply risk (availability):

| | Low supply risk | High supply risk |
|---|----------------|------------------|
| **High importance** | Leverage items — buyers push price, multi-source | Strategic items — partnership, joint development |
| **Low importance** | Non-critical — simplify, automate | Bottleneck items — secure supply, buyer anxious |

Know which quadrant your offer sits in for the customer, and sell accordingly (a strategic supplier co-develops; a leverage supplier must win on total value to avoid pure price competition).

### 7. Move up the relationship ladder

Decide the target relationship and build toward it. The strategic direction in B2B is from **transactional** (each deal standalone, price-led, low switching cost, short horizon) to **relational** (durable relationship, quality/service-led, high interdependence and switching cost, mutual investment, long horizon). A relationship is "advanced" when: both sides esteem each other beyond price; they run joint activities beyond pure selling; they hold scheduled reviews; the relationship endures; the supplier has a dedicated account subsystem; and the customer is prioritized (and vice versa). Use the leverage/dependence matrix (customer vs. supplier power) to pick the realistic relationship model — strategic alliance, buyer-dependent, supplier-dependent, or market exchange.

## Output

### B2B Context
The structural factors (derived demand, concentration, procedural buying, continuity) that shape this specific case.

### Buying Center Map
Roles → people → goals/criteria → message & proof → how to reach each.

### Buy-Class & Process
The situation classified, the process stages, and your action at each — with the earliest high-leverage intervention flagged.

### Uncertainty-Reduction Plan
The three uncertainties for this buyer and how you'll reduce each.

### Procurement & Relationship Position
Kraljic quadrant, the relationship target, and the plan to advance it.

## Follow-up

Ask: "Would you like me to:
- Design an account-based marketing plan to reach this buying center?
- Build sales-enablement content mapped to the buying process stages?
- Develop a key-account relationship / retention plan?
- Set the communication plan to reach the influencers sales can't?"
