# Distribution & Channel Strategy

Distribution is the system that moves not just the product but also information and money between producer and customer — and back. In modern B2B it is as much about the *information flow* (specs, configuration, contracts) as the physical flow. Channel decisions are semi-permanent and shape reach, cost, control, and even positioning, so they deserve deliberate design rather than default.

## Trigger

User asks how to distribute or sell through, channel design/selection, direct vs. indirect, intermediaries, push vs. pull, sell-in/sell-out, omnichannel/e-commerce channels, channel conflict, or trade/partner marketing.

## Inputs

1. **Product/service** — technical complexity, customization, order size, service needs
2. **Target segment** and buying behavior (pull from STP / `b2b-buying-behavior`)
3. **Positioning** — exclusive distribution signals premium; intensive signals mass
4. **Current channels** (if any) and their performance
5. **Resources and control appetite** — direct = control + cost; indirect = reach + less control

## Process

### 1. Understand the three flows

Design for all three, not just the truck:
- **Physical flow** — the goods themselves (logistics, storage, delivery, installation)
- **Information flow** — specs, needs, configuration, proposals, contracts, support (dominant when the product is complex or customized)
- **Financial flow** — payment, credit, financing (often involves third parties)

The more customized and technically complex the offer, the heavier the information flow and the stronger the case for direct or high-touch channels.

### 2. Choose channel type and length

- **Direct** (producer → customer): maximum control over price, message, and relationship; needed for high customization, large orders, and technical selling. Typical for small firms early on and for key B2B accounts.
- **Indirect** (producer → intermediaries → customer): intermediaries exist because they add efficiency — geographic reach, assortment/critical mass, specialization, and transaction economy. Length options:
  - Zero-level (direct)
  - One-level (producer → retailer/dealer → customer)
  - Two-level (producer → wholesaler → retailer → customer)

Longer channels extend reach but reduce control and margin, and distance you from the end customer — whose preferences you must still influence *through* the intermediaries.

### 3. Set channel intensity

Match coverage to the product and position:

| Intensity | Coverage | Fits |
|-----------|----------|------|
| Intensive | Many outlets, maximum availability | Convenience/consumable goods, "on the shelf" purchases |
| Selective | Chosen specialists, medium coverage | Products needing some expertise/service; balances reach and control |
| Exclusive | Few, single-brand partners | Premium/technical goods; protects image, margin, and support quality |

### 4. Pick intermediary types (B2B especially)

- **Distributors** — buy and resell, take title and risk (credit, obsolescence); exclusive or multi-brand
- **Agents** — sell in the producer's name for commission; don't take title; producer invoices and ships
- **Brokers** — broker deals between producer and buyer; paid by one or both sides
- **OEM / VAR** — incorporate your product into theirs (visibly, for VAR); installers, integrators, software houses
- **Digital marketplaces / platforms** — reach and data, but disintermediation and price-transparency risks

### 5. Allocate functions along the channel

Distribution is a bundle of jobs that must be done by *someone*; decide who performs and who is paid for each: market information, promotion, technical consulting, customer contact, adaptation (quantity/quality), negotiation, physical distribution (transport, storage, installation), financing/collection, and risk-bearing. Channel margins should map to the functions each party performs — this is the logic behind functional discounts.

### 6. Choose push vs. pull

- **Push** — direct effort at the channel; the distributor "pushes" the product downstream. Producer supports with trade margins, training, logistics. Fits selective channels and products that need installing/adapting (OEM). More active distributor, higher trade margins.
- **Pull** — direct effort at the end customer, who then "pulls" the product through the channel by demanding it. Producer invests in advertising/promotion to end users. Fits intensive channels and finished goods. Distributor is reactive.
- Most real strategies blend both; state the balance and why.

### 7. Manage omnichannel and digital

B2B e-commerce has grown far faster than B2C in recent years and buyers increasingly expect it. Distinguish:
- **Multichannel** — several channels operating independently
- **Omnichannel** — all channels coordinated into one seamless experience (shared data, consistent pricing/service)

Benefits of true omnichannel: better sales-process visibility, richer data for channel optimization, stronger partner relationships, and higher efficiency/productivity. Plan direct online (own site), specialized distribution sites, and marketplaces alongside physical channels — and coordinate their pricing to avoid channel conflict.

### 8. Trade marketing (managing the channel as a customer)

The intermediary is itself a customer to be marketed to. **Trade marketing** = activities aimed at getting the trade to stock, position, and push your products: joint business planning, budgeting, promotional menus, joint data analysis, and relationship building. Negotiate assortment inclusion and shelf/portfolio position; align objectives with the channel rather than dictating.

### 9. Resolve channel conflict

Anticipate conflict from overlapping channels (e.g., direct online undercutting distributors). Manage with clear role/segment boundaries, consistent pricing across channels, and compensation that rewards the functions each channel performs.

## Output

### Channel Design
Type (direct/indirect), length, and intensity — each justified against product, buyer behavior, and positioning.

### Intermediary Plan
Which intermediary types, their roles, and the function/margin allocation table.

### Push/Pull Strategy
The balance chosen and the supporting investments.

### Omnichannel & Digital
The channel portfolio (physical + direct online + marketplaces) and the coordination/anti-conflict rules.

### Trade Marketing
How the channel partners will be won and managed (sell-in) and how sell-out will be driven.

### Risks
Conflict, control loss, disintermediation — and mitigations.

- Align channel pricing across direct and indirect (pocket price by channel)?
- Design the trade-marketing / partner program in detail?
- Build the omnichannel coordination plan?
- Fit distribution into the full marketing mix and plan?"
