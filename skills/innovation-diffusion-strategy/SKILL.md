# Innovation Diffusion & Adoption Strategy

New offerings don't sell like established ones — they spread through a *social process of adoption* with distinct segments that buy for different reasons and must be won in sequence. Treating the mainstream like early adopters (or vice versa) is why promising innovations stall. This skill applies diffusion theory to forecast adoption and to design the stage-appropriate moves that carry an innovation from first users to mass market.

## Trigger

User launches or scales a new/innovative product, service, or technology; needs an adoption forecast; faces a stall between early adopters and the mainstream; or asks about adopter segments, the chasm, disruption, technology substitution, or the product life cycle of something novel.

## Inputs

1. **The innovation** — what's new about it; continuous (incremental) or discontinuous (requires behavior change)?
2. **Where it is now** — pre-launch, early users only, stalling, scaling
3. **Adopter evidence** — who's buying today and why
4. **Market** — B2B or B2C; size and comparable products
5. **Competitive/technology context** — substitutes, incumbents, standards

## Process

### 1. Classify the innovation

The type sets the whole strategy:
- **Continuous / incremental** — fits existing behavior; diffuses relatively smoothly along the S-curve.
- **Discontinuous / really-new** — demands new behavior or infrastructure; adoption is slower, chasm risk is high, and the *ecosystem* (complements, standards) often gates the pace.

Also locate it on the **technology S-curve**: early (slow, immature), steep (rapid improvement), or maturing (diminishing returns) — and watch for a **discontinuity** where a new technology S-curve replaces the old. Substitution pace depends on both the new tech's emergence challenge and the old tech's remaining headroom.

### 2. Read the adopter categories (Rogers)

Adoption follows a bell curve of segments that buy for *different reasons* — the heart of diffusion strategy:

| Segment | ~Share | Buys because | How to win them |
|---------|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Innovators | 2.5% | Love the new; tech for its own sake | Access, transparency, co-development |
| Early adopters | 13.5% | Vision; competitive advantage from being first | Business case, bold outcomes, references from peers |
| Early majority | 34% | Pragmatism; proven productivity | Whole product, references, low risk, standards |
| Late majority | 34% | Pressure; don't want to be left behind | Ease, support, established leader, price |
| Laggards | 16% | Only when forced | Simplicity, no alternative left |

The five determinants of *adoption speed* (Rogers): **relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, observability** — improve these to accelerate diffusion. Add: novelty level, fit to needs, presence of substitutes, marketing effort, positioning, and brand strength.

### 3. Find and cross the chasm (Moore)

The dangerous gap is between **early adopters** (visionaries, buy on vision) and the **early majority** (pragmatists, buy on proven whole solutions and peer references). Their buying logic is so different that momentum stalls — the **chasm**. To cross it:
- **Target a single beachhead niche** — a specific pragmatist segment with a compelling reason to buy now, small enough to dominate.
- **Deliver the "whole product"** — everything needed to fully solve that niche's problem (integrations, support, services), not just the core.
- **Win references within the niche** — pragmatists trust peers, not visionaries; dominate one niche to earn them, then expand to adjacent niches (the "bowling pin" path).
- Reposition from "exciting and new" to "proven and safe."

### 4. Forecast adoption (Bass model)

Quantify the adoption curve by separating two forces: **innovation** (external influence — advertising, p) and **imitation** (internal influence — word of mouth, q). The Bass model produces the classic S-shaped cumulative curve and its bell-shaped adoption rate, and estimates time-to-peak and peak sales. Use it to plan capacity, spend, and expectations. See `references/diffusion-models.md` for the **full set of four diffusion-curve models** (S-curve, bell curve, linear trajectories, shark fin) plus the Bass equations, parameter meaning, estimation, and worked interpretation.

### 5. Disruption as a diffusion model — linear trajectories (Christensen)

The **linear-trajectories** model plots performance against time to explain why an apparently inferior innovation can overtake established ones. Technology usually improves *faster* than customers can absorb, so a sustaining technology eventually **overshoots** even the high-end market's needs — opening room underneath. A **disruptive innovation** enters *below* mainstream-demanded performance (cheaper, simpler, "not good enough" at first), takes root in the low end or a *new* market that values different attributes, then climbs its own trajectory until it rises to meet mainstream demand and displaces incumbents who fled up-market chasing margin. Implications:
- As a challenger: don't fight incumbents head-on; enter where they're happy to cede (low end / non-consumers / a new use), compete on a *different* performance dimension, then climb.
- As an incumbent: watch trajectories, not today's ranking; a worse-on-paper entrant with a steeper improvement trajectory wins while you over-serve.
- Distinguish **sustaining** innovation (better products for your best existing customers) — which favors incumbents — from **disruptive**, which favors attackers from below. See the reference file for the full model.

### 6. Compressed diffusion — the shark fin / "devastating" disruption (Downes & Nunes)

For many digital, networked, and platform innovations the smooth bell curve collapses into a **shark fin**: a near-vertical rise (almost everyone adopts almost at once) followed by an almost-as-steep fall. It is sometimes called *devastating* innovation because incumbents get no gradual ramp to react to. Rogers' five segments collapse into essentially two — a brief pool of **trial users**, then **everybody else** — driven by instant information, products that are better *and* cheaper from day one, and network effects. Its four stages: **Singularity** (cheap experiments) → **Big Bang** (explosive adoption) → **Big Crunch** (sharp collapse) → **Entropy** (aftermath). Plan for compressed, often winner-take-all cycles: experiment continuously, scale instantly behind whatever ignites, and reinvent or exit before the crunch rather than defending one S-curve.

Innovations still ride the underlying **product life cycle** (introduction → growth → maturity → decline), with the marketing job shifting by stage (trial → preference → loyalty → harvest) — but choose the curve shape (S-curve, linear trajectories, or shark fin) that actually fits, and set expectations and reinvestment cadence accordingly. See the reference file for all four models.

### 7. Set the stage-appropriate plan

Translate the diagnosis into moves: which adopter segment to target now, how to accelerate the five adoption determinants, the whole-product gaps to close, the beachhead niche (if crossing the chasm), the launch pricing logic (skim for innovators/early adopters vs. penetrate for the majority — hand to `pricing-strategy`), and the reinvention plan for the next S-curve.

## Output

### Innovation Diagnosis
Type (continuous/discontinuous, sustaining/disruptive), S-curve position, and current adopter segment.

### Adoption Map
Rogers segments with who you have vs. need next; the five adoption determinants scored with acceleration levers.

### Chasm Assessment
Whether a chasm looms, the beachhead niche, and the whole-product gaps to close.

### Adoption Forecast
Bass-model-based curve (parameters, time-to-peak, peak) with conservative/base/optimistic ranges — see the reference file for math.

### Stage Plan
The prioritized, adopter-appropriate moves and the reinvention/next-curve plan.
