# Segmentation, Targeting & Positioning (STP)

STP is the bridge from market analysis to the marketing mix. You cannot design product, price, place, or promotion until you know *who* you serve and *what position* you want to own in their mind. This skill runs the full sequence: segment → target → position, ending in a positioning statement and a perceptual map that the whole mix must then serve coherently.

## Trigger

User asks for segmentation, target market selection, personas/ICP, positioning, repositioning, differentiation, or perceptual/positioning maps — or is about to design a marketing mix, launch, or campaign without a defined target and position.

## Inputs

1. **Market / category** and the offering
2. **B2B or B2C** — determines the segmentation bases
3. **What's known about customers** — needs, behaviors, firmographics, current buyers
4. **Business goal** — grow share, enter a niche, defend, launch
5. **Competitors and how customers currently perceive them** (for positioning)
6. **Constraints** — resources, brand permission, channel reach

## Process

### 1. Qualify the market first

Before segmenting, size the funnel of demand so segments are chosen against real potential, not the whole population:

- **Potential market** — all who could ever buy if offer/marketing were maximized
- **Available (qualified) market** — those with interest, income, and access (and any required qualifications)
- **Served (target) market** — the part of the qualified market you choose to pursue
- **Penetrated market** — those already buying (your current customers)

The strategic question — grow by conquering non-users, taking share from rivals, or deepening current customers — falls out of this framing. Name it.

### 2. Choose segmentation bases

Segment into groups that are internally homogeneous and externally heterogeneous in ways that matter for the offer. Pick bases that predict differing *needs or responses*, not just describe people.

**B2C bases:**
- Geographic — region, climate, urban/rural, market maturity
- Demographic — age, income, gender, life stage, education
- Psychographic — values, lifestyle, attitudes, personality
- Behavioral — usage rate, occasion, benefits sought, loyalty, buying stage (often the most powerful)

**B2B / industrial bases (nested, macro → micro):**
- Firmographic — industry (SIC/NACE), company size, geography, technology level, OEM vs. end-user
- Operating — usage volume, capabilities required, purchasing policy
- Purchasing approach — centralized/decentralized, buying criteria, existing relationships, buy-class
- Situational — urgency, order size, application
- Personal characteristics of buyers — risk attitude, loyalty, decision style

**Method note:** two broad approaches exist — statistical (factor/cluster/conjoint analysis) when you have data, and heuristic/successive-elimination (Porter) when you don't: list candidate bases, pair the significant ones two-at-a-time into a product/market matrix, drop the meaningless cells, and keep the segments that remain. Prefer needs-based segmentation when possible; it is more actionable than pure descriptors. The energy-efficiency case is the archetype: segmenting residential users by *attitude and behavior* (e.g., "green advocates," "cost-focused savers," "disengaged wasters") beat demographic segmentation because it predicted response to messaging.

### 3. Test segment quality

Keep a segment only if it passes all five tests — otherwise it is a statistical artifact, not a market:

| Test | Question |
|------|----------|
| Measurable | Can you size it and its purchasing power? |
| Accessible | Can you reach it via channels/communication at reasonable cost? |
| Substantial | Is it big/profitable enough to justify a tailored program (covers the incremental cost)? |
| Differentiable | Does it respond differently from other segments? (If not, don't split.) |
| Actionable | Can you actually design and execute a distinct program for it? |
| Durable | Will it persist long enough to earn back the investment? |

### 4. Evaluate and select targets

Score segments on **attractiveness** (size, growth, profitability, competitive intensity, fit with strengths — reuse Five Forces per segment) against **ability to serve** (capabilities, channel access, differentiation potential). Then choose a targeting pattern:

| Pattern | Description | Fits |
|---------|-------------|------|
| Undifferentiated (mass) | One offer for the whole market | Commodity, huge scale (rare today) |
| Differentiated | Distinct offers for several segments | Resource-rich firms, mature markets |
| Concentrated (niche) | One segment, deeply served | Focused firms, startups, premium |
| Micromarketing / one-to-one | Tailored to individual accounts | B2B key accounts, high-value custom |
| Product / market / selective specialization | Own a product across segments, a segment across products, or cherry-pick cells | Portfolio choices |

State which segments are in, which are out, and why.

### 5. Position within the target

Positioning is the place the offer occupies in the *customer's mind* relative to competitors — decided before anything is built. Construct it deliberately:

1. **Identify determinant attributes** — the 2 axes customers actually use to judge and choose in this category (areas of need, not features you like). These become the perceptual-map axes.
2. **Map current perceptions** — plot competitors (and yourself) on those axes to reveal crowded zones and open space.
3. **Choose a position** — a spot that is relevant to the target, credible for you, differentiated from rivals, and durable.
4. **Define the frame of reference** — which category you're competing in (reframing the category can be more powerful than fighting inside it).

Write a **positioning statement**:

> For [target segment] who [need/occasion], [brand] is the [frame of reference/category] that [key benefit/point of difference], because [reason to believe / proof].

Example:
> For mid-market SaaS marketing teams drowning in disconnected tools, Acme is the campaign platform that unifies planning and execution in one workspace, because it runs on a single data model that eliminates tool fragmentation.

### 6. Check positioning quality

A strong position is: aimed at a defined segment; anchored to a mental category the segment already holds; relevant; focused (1-2 differentiators, not many); simple and unambiguous; offering a superior/exclusive perceived benefit; durable; and coherent across every mix element. Reject positions that are generic, feature-led, or that fight the category's existing mental model without reason.

### Repositioning (when triggered)

Reposition only for a deep, lasting shift in customers or a truly strategic competitor move — not a tactical blip. Preserve continuity cues (name, codes, heritage) so the brand stays recognizable; abrupt breaks erase hard-won memory. Map the current position, the desired position, and the migration path.

## Output

### Market Qualification
The potential → available → served → penetrated funnel and the growth strategy implied.

### Segmentation
The bases chosen (with rationale), the resulting segments profiled, and the five-test screen.

### Targeting
Attractiveness × ability-to-serve assessment, the chosen pattern, and the in/out decision.

### Positioning
- Perceptual map (describe axes + placements; render as a simple 2×2 if useful)
- Positioning statement (filled template)
- Quality check against the criteria
- Points of difference vs. points of parity

### Handoff
What the marketing mix must now deliver to earn this position.

## Follow-up

Ask: "Would you like me to:
- Design the marketing mix (4P/7P) to deliver this positioning?
- Build the value proposition for the chosen target in detail?
- Draft the messaging and campaign for the target segment?
- Produce a repositioning migration plan?"
